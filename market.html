Market & Model

Three modes. Three markets. One wedge.

The career stack was built for a labor market that no longer exists. AI broke the signals, the career center is unstaffed, and the resume is a filter instead of a signal. Dilly is the first connected career system designed for that reality, and the first that runs economically at a student price point.

The Shift

The career stack wasn't built for any of this.

Every surface between a person and their job was built for a world that no longer exists. AI broke the old signals. The people trying to navigate it no longer trust them. The ones getting replaced don't have time to figure it out alone.

01

Career support is a lottery.

Students get one advisor per 2,263 of them. Mid-career professionals get zero. The only people with real career infrastructure are the already-advantaged.

02

The resume is a filter, not a signal.

75% of applications are rejected by an ATS before a human sees them. The survivors get a six-second skim. Nobody learns anything, not the applicant, not the employer.

03

AI broke scoring. Now it threatens the job.

Every resume reads like a consultant wrote it. Every cover letter is GPT-polished. Meanwhile, 30 to 70% of roles in most cohorts face meaningful AI disruption in the next five years. The tools people relied on can't help with what's actually happening.

Category Creation

Not a better tool. A new category.

Billion-dollar outcomes in this space don't come from out-competing Jobscan at resume scanning. They come from building the product the next generation expects to exist. Dilly is building the category, not competing in one.

Yesterday (2000–2020)

The Resume Era

The document was the unit. Jobscan, VMock, Handshake, Indeed all built around scanning, scoring, posting, and matching a resume. Every tool optimized for the same piece of paper that fewer and fewer people were actually reading. The career center existed to help students fix that document.

Today (2024–)

The GPT Collapse

ChatGPT writes the resume. ChatGPT writes the cover letter. ATS vendors build AI to reject the AI-written applications. The document has become noise. No competitor has repositioned. They all still sell resume-adjacent products to a market where the resume no longer signals anything.

Dilly (2026+)

The Profile Era

The unit is the person, not the document. A living, structured, conversation-built record that travels across every career stage. Fit narratives replace scores. Disruption intelligence replaces certainty. The profile works across the student's whole life, and the category is defined around that. Dilly ships it.

The Math

We don't need to be everything to everyone. We need to become the default for the person who takes their career seriously, then hold them across every status change for the next forty years.

The Question Every Investor Asks

What happens when LinkedIn ships an AI career coach?

They will. Here is why it doesn't matter and why LinkedIn building it might actually be the best thing that could happen to Dilly.

LinkedIn's data is a highlight reel. Dilly's data is the truth.

LinkedIn profiles are curated for public consumption. Users put their best roles, their cleanest titles, their most impressive bullets. Nobody puts their fears on LinkedIn. Nobody says they hate cold outreach and have no idea if their target companies are realistic. Dilly's profile captures all of that because it's private, conversational, and structured to hold the real picture. An AI career coach trained on LinkedIn data is an AI that only knows the polished version of who you are. That's not coaching. That's flattery.

LinkedIn's business model is structurally opposed to this feature.

LinkedIn makes money when recruiters pay for access to candidates, and when companies pay for job listings. An AI that helps users get jobs faster and negotiate better offers directly compresses that revenue cycle. Fewer applications per hire means fewer premium listing clicks. Better-matched candidates means less recruiter seat usage. LinkedIn's incentives are to keep job seekers searching, not to place them quickly. Dilly's entire model inverts that. We win when the user wins.

LinkedIn cannot build a private profile. That's the whole architecture.

The Dilly profile works because users trust it with information they'd never make public. The architecture is private-by-default, opt-in for visibility. LinkedIn is the opposite: public-by-default, privacy is the exception. You can't bolt a private journal onto a public social network and expect users to actually fill it in honestly. The profile architecture isn't a feature LinkedIn can copy. It requires a ground-up rethink of what the product is which means abandoning what made LinkedIn valuable to its existing users.

Students don't use LinkedIn. That's the wedge market.

LinkedIn's weakest demographic is exactly Dilly's primary market. College students open LinkedIn to send one application and close it. They don't maintain profiles, they don't engage, and they don't trust it with anything real about where they're headed. Dilly is purpose-built for the pre-LinkedIn user the person who doesn't have a professional identity yet and needs help building one. By the time LinkedIn tries to serve students, Dilly already owns that relationship. And those students graduate into the Holder mode with years of profile depth that LinkedIn will never recover.

The honest answer is that LinkedIn entering this category is validation. It means the category is real, the TAM is large, and the problem is worth solving. Every time a big company tries to serve a market with a bolt-on feature, it opens a window for a focused competitor to own the users who want the real thing. Dilly is the real thing.
Defensibility

Four moats, none of them marketing.

The hard part of this business isn't acquisition. It's compounding value and controlled costs. Dilly is built so every user makes the product harder to copy and cheaper to run.

✦

The profile is the moat.

Every conversation, every edit, every uploaded resume deepens a structured record that is unique to the user and non-portable. Switching cost compounds with time-in-product, and because users stay through every career status change, that compounding never resets.

Ten products sharing one engine is the moat.

Every competitor is a single-surface tool: a resume scanner, a job board, a comp benchmark. Dilly reuses the same profile, prompts, and infrastructure across ten verticals. Each new vertical ships in weeks, not years. Competitors trying to catch up would have to build ten products.

Cost efficiency is the moat.

Haiku 4.5 exclusively. Prompt caching. Batch generation. Static lookup tables for AI-disruption data. $1.50/user/mo LLM spend at the current baseline. Every competitor running a Sonnet or Opus backbone cannot hit our price point without burning on every call.

AI Arena is the moat.

No competitor in this category ships AI-vulnerability scanning, replace-tests, or disruption intelligence. It's the only reason a user opens a career app when they aren't actively job-hunting, and the only defensive product for a market where the job itself is under threat.
