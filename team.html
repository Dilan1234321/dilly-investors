Founder

Founder · Built the Whole Thing

20. Data Science at the University of Tampa. Started Dilly because the career center was unreachable, the resume scanners were useless, and the AI tools everyone else was shipping were all pointed in the wrong direction: toward polishing a document nobody reads.

Since then: shipped iOS build 301+ to TestFlight, stood up 50+ FastAPI routers, wrote the cost ledger that keeps LLM spend under $1.50/user/mo, integrated Stripe across three checkout flows, built 22 cohorts × 18 path-aware advisor lenses, launched Skill Lab v0.1, and moved the product from a single-mode resume tool into a three-mode career OS with a six-product roadmap behind it.

Dilly is profile-first because it's personal. The people this product helps are the ones who didn't get the career-center appointment, didn't get the referral, didn't know what "Grit" was supposed to measure anyway. It's for them.

Why Now

The product and the moment arrived together.

Three things converged to make this the right build at the right time. The reason a solo founder got it this far is because the cost-per-call finally makes a profile-first AI career platform economically possible.

AI disruption is the demand.

30 to 70% of roles in most cohorts face meaningful AI disruption in five years. The people in those roles know it. There is no existing product for a career-worried knowledge worker, just generic resume scanners. That vacuum is the opening.

Haiku 4.5 is the unlock.

Two years ago, running fit narratives plus a What-We-Think letter plus cohort pulses plus AI-vulnerability scans on a student-priced subscription was impossible. Haiku 4.5 (with caching and batching) made it run at $1.50/user/mo. The economics only exist now.

The category is empty.

Jobscan is ATS scanning. VMock is resume scoring. Handshake is a job board. ChatGPT is a blank page. Nobody has shipped a profile-first, mode-aware, AI-defense-aware career OS. We got here first, and we got here because no incumbent could rebuild around the new economics fast enough.

The distribution is proven.

Universities are overwhelmed and looking for career-tech partners. A launch at the University of Tampa, expansion across the SEC, and the first 25 school partnerships represent ~750,000 addressable students by month eighteen. A realistic, nameable path, not a TAM deck.
